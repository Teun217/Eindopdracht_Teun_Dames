Subtractive Synthesis

A very common type of synthesizer is the subtractive synthesizer. Subtractive synthesis basicallyl means that there is a little screamer (the oscillator) it makes a lot of noise. The job of the instrumentalist is to cut down, subtract, from that sound to form a new sound. On this page I am going to show a couple of features regarding this on the Deepmind 12.

Features on the Deepmind

Oscillators

Oscillators are the things that make the noise within a synthesizer.

Oscillator 1

You have two types of waves within this synth. Saw waves, which you can hear through the first audio sample. And square waves, which you can hear through the second audio sample. You can also combine these waves.

Oscillator 2

The deepmind has 2 oscillators that can play over eachother. Oscillator 2 gives you among other things control over pitch and volume. The first oscillator doesn't have pitch or volume control directly with slider. The second oscillator produces a square wave. The first audio clip lets you hear oscillator 1 and then oscillator 2 playing over it.

Noise level

This slider regulates the volume of the noise. It can be used to add texture to a sound.

VCF (Voltage Controlled low pass Filter)

This filter lets lower frequencies through and filters away higher ones.

frequency control

One of the two controls you have within the VCF is the frequency control. This controls at which level of frequency the sound gets cut. In the audio sample you can here the higher frequencies going away and back again. This, I think, results in a sharper tone. With the frequency all the way down, it sound like you're underwater.

resonandce control

Achieved by applying FEEDBACK to a FILTER: the effect of resonance “boosts” the frequencies around the CUTOFF frequency. As resonance increases, the feedback increases to a point that the filter can generate a sine-wave tone.

HPF (high pass filter)

This slider filters lower frequencies away. This you can hear within the audio sample: the sound get less "full".

Envelopes

Envelopes are a way of controlling the volume or certain filters within the synth. They are basically telling the synth how much, volume or control over other filter, it should give. The following envelopes are in order next to each other. All of the audio samples show control over volume, but you can also link envelopes to different filters.

Attack

Attack decides how long it takes for the filter to reach its max level.

Decay

Decay decides how long it takes for the filter to lower to sustain level.

Sustain

Sustain is the level the filter stays at after the decay. The sustain lasts as long as the note is still being pressed.

Release

Release decides how long it takes for a note to fade out after the key has been released. So with a sustain of 30 seconds, after you release the key that plays the note, the note will fade out over 30 seconds.

Arpeggiator

An arpeggiator makes it so if you press multiple notes the get played after eachother, this can be in different patterns. But what happens a lot is just going down all the notes that are pressed, like in the audio sample, or going up all the notes that are pressed. The arpeggiator can be sped up or slowed down, this is also hearable in the audio clip.

Low Frequency Oscillators (LFO)

In my opinion maybe one of the more interesting sliders on the synth are the LFO's. As the name suggests they are oscillators. But instead of having a frequency so high it forms a tone, it's slow. You can connect a LFO to almost every filter (and more) within the deepmind. For example connect it to level (volume) or pitch control for a slight vibrato. In the audio clip the oscillator is between volume all the way off to all the way on. This doesn't sound that good (but I thought it was helpful to demonstrate), so usually you tune it so it's less "deep".
